# Advanced Failure Notes

This document collects working notes on advanced fictional-dialogue failures that are often easier to recognize than to score.

These failures matter because they explain why advanced models can produce text that is locally plausible, grammatically clean, and emotionally literate while still feeling false to experienced readers.

## General Pattern

Many advanced failures appear when the model quietly optimizes for one or more of the following:

- readability
- symmetry
- coherence
- explicitness
- aesthetic polish
- reader comfort
- recoverability after heavy moments

These optimizations are not always wrong in ordinary writing. They become problematic when they override scene truth.

## Reader-Sensitive Diagnostic Questions

When examining advanced failures, ask:

- Does this scene feel too balanced?
- Does this character feel too self-consistent?
- Has desire been made too easy to read?
- Has vulnerability appeared without friction?
- Has the text started to sound like a template rather than an occurrence?
- Has the impact of a heavy line or action been softened too quickly?
- Is the text describing the experience instead of letting it happen?
- Has the pacing flattened across scenes that should feel very different in time?

## Typical Reader Reactions

Advanced failures often trigger reactions such as:

- "nothing is technically wrong, but this feels fake"
- "the model is already smoothing it"
- "this is too neat"
- "it turned readable instead of real"
- "why is every scene moving at the same speed"
- "it already explained too much"
- "this is writing about the scene, not being in it"

These reactions should be treated as usable analytic data rather than dismissed as vague taste.
